Caesar

You probably heard of the Caesar method before, because it can be considered one of the most widely known method, therefore not very effective at protecting a message.
This method consists in assigning letters with a number based on their alphabetical order (A=1; …; Z=26) and increasing or decreasing all the letters by a number. We call this an increment or a shift.
For example, with an increment of 19, A becomes T, B becomes U, and Z becomes S (you just need to subtract 26 in case, after the shift, the number is higher than 26)
To decrypt a Caesar code, you could just try all the possible increments, which in the end don’t result in more than 25 different combinations (Since a shift of 26 is basically the message itself, we don't count it).
If you don’t want to randomly try all the combinations, you could analyze the text and check things like the most used letter or word. In the English language, the most used letters are “E”, “T” and “A”. Also, if there is a 3 letters word in the text, it is very likely that this word is either “and” or “the”.
By working with all these clues you can get by analyzing the text, you can find the right shift much faster compared to brute-forcingThe method of "brute-forcing" consist in trying all possible combinations until you find the right one. your way through.
