Conclusion

We saw that in course of centuries, the ways we used encryption to secure our messages have improved of an exponential manner, until now where they became nearly impossible to bypass, such as Hash functions or even the Vigenere code, which is still really hard to decrypt despite it old age if you don’t have the key.

We have noticed that the improvement of the encrypting methods is hugely linked to the improvement of the encryption keys. Indeed, it’s the combination between the message and its key that give the encrypted message, so, the more complex is the key, the more complex is the message. Encryption started with the Caesar code, using a key with 25 possibilities. Then we went through the Vigenere code, with a key with “26 power the numbers of letters in the key” possibilities. We now have hash functions, nearly foolproof, reducing the coded message at a chain of lettering of the same size. Also, in our everyday life, we have the “simple” password, that doesn’t directly encrypt the text, but adds security to deny the possibility to read it, with “95 power the number of letters in the password” possibilities.

However, despite all those more or less extremely secured ways, security failures still exist, because any logical algorithm created by the human mind cannot be totally foolproof.

So, in the end, we can say that the best way to secure our data is to use a Hash function, like SHA-256 for example. But since the hash function can be pretty difficult to understand and to use by a total beginner, we can also suggest the Vigenere code, which is pretty secured if use a long key (like a quote from a movie or video game character that you like and are sure you won’t forget).
